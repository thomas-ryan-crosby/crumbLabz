1 Client Overview

Company: Promondo Sports LLC (styled “ProMondo Sports”; legal name confirmed on client invoices). The name is Italian-derived — pro mondo, “pro world.” During the call Nic floated rebranding to “ProMondo Sports Group / PSG,” but explicitly noted “this isn't actually going to happen, that's just me being a copycat” — treat the rebrand as aspirational, not a requirement.

Industry: Basketball player representation / sports agency — full-service athlete management spanning recruiting, contract/agency representation, NIL and brand/marketing deals, performance tracking, and high-touch personal/executive services for players and their families.

  • No real web presence: “you could technically go on the website and it's just a cover page, there's nothing to do on there” (promondo.com / promondosports.com).

2 Problem Statement

ProMondo Sports is a fast-growing basketball agency representing dozens of professional, international, and collegiate athletes — and recruiting hundreds more — yet it runs entirely on a patchwork of shared Google Sheets, individual desktop folders, personal Gmail accounts, phone contacts/notes, and ad-hoc group chats, with no centralized system of record. Critical, sensitive player data (passports, driver's licenses, Social Security cards, birth certificates, combine measurements, contracts, NIL deals, even a marquee client's credit card) lives scattered across one person's laptop and phone, so Nic Meister — the firm's most organized operator — has become the involuntary single point of knowledge: “anytime [someone] needs a… address, passport, license… [they send] it in a group chat, and then just wait for somebody to send it.” The current tools fail because they have no permissions model (finances must be hidden from junior staff, but everything else should be shared), no de-duplication or coordination safeguards (two agents repeatedly track and risk contacting the same recruit — visible in the workbook itself), no enforced process for eligibility-critical workflows like NBA draft declaration (a 5–7-step sequence “nobody really knows… except me”), no mobile access despite a phone-first team, and no client-facing layer — all of which gets exponentially harder as the firm scales toward servicing high-profile NBA clients like Keaton Wagler.

3 Current Workflow

Recruiting & Pipeline Tracking

  • A master Google Sheet workbook (ProMondo Sports Basketball 25-26) holds a Legend & Key tab (status + tier definitions), a Master Client List (signed roster grouped by tier: NBA → G-League → Euro → NCAA → HS), and one recruiting tab per agent.
  • When an agent identifies a target, they post the name in a main group chat with everyone; the team waits for confirmation that no one else is already on that player (“juice”/contact) to avoid overlap.
  • The agent then adds the prospect to their own named tab with status (Signed, Committed, Recruiting, Target, Lead, Watching, Poaching, etc.), school/circuit, ranking, height/weight, position, “Who Has Juice?”, player/parent contacts, social handle, and dated notes.
  • Disorganized agents instead hand prospects off to “one of our organized intern/operations people” to enter.
  • One named task that week: “clean it up from everything that's happened since after the draft… and make it organized” — the recruiting sheet is acknowledged as out of date.

Player Information & Document Storage

  • Player records live as folders on Nic's personal desktop, organized by level: college, high school, international, NBA.
  • Each player folder follows a consistent taxonomy (confirmed by the Fletcher Loyer example shared): Contracts (agency/NBPA agreement, marketing rep agreement), Finances (W-9), Invoices, NIL (per-brand subfolders — Mizzen+Main, Uber Eats, Fanatics, Fifth Third Bank, Postgame, etc.), Player Info (measurements, sizes, SSN, birth certificate), Pre-Draft / Combine (applications, health authorizations, school disclosures), and Travel Info (passport, ID/license).
  • Player contact details and sizes are split between phone contacts/notes and the sheet notes column.
  • For the incoming Wagler client, Nic compiled a single PDF “family servicing sheet” (names, birthdays, emails, flight numbers, credit card) so any staffer can transact on the player's behalf.
  • The firm intended to move all of this to OneDrive “this fall” but has not started.

Performance Tracking & Investor Reporting

  • Live game tracking via consumer sports apps (CBS Sports for college/pro) “like anybody would.”
  • A previous automation emailed 5 AM stat digests of every represented player's prior-night performance to investors/staff; it was custom-coded, has become unreliable, and someone is rebuilding it “with Claude in their free time.”
  • High-school stats are gathered manually by a designated staffer (“I have someone else do that… I can't find that stuff”).
  • For high-volume nights, the firm maintains a game-watching schedule Google Sheet; 8–10 interns sign up and post quarter-by-quarter updates to a group chat (also flagging injuries).
  • Twitter is used to catch noteworthy/injury news for higher-profile players. Birthdays are kept in calendars.
  • (Reference model) CrumbLabz already built Josh's “Fringe” an Investor Reporting Dashboard — paste financials + a blurb → ready-to-send report; “a day's-long task takes me like 10 minutes now.”

Invoicing & Payments

  • Invoices are generated from an accounting email via a Claude project template: staffer supplies player name, amount, player email, description, and a pasted Stripe payment link → Claude produces the invoice → it's emailed. (Wire details are also offered as an alternative.)
  • Payments run through Stripe (~$5 per paid invoice) for visibility on incoming funds.
  • A separate payments Google Sheet (college) tracks, per player: total money they're making, total being invoiced, payment schedule, and month-by-month amounts owed/when, marked as invoiced.

Logistics / “Spreadsheet Bridges”

  • For big events (NBA Draft, Summer League, Combine), Nic spins up Claude-generated tracking sheets — e.g., 8–12 people traveling: who goes which day, flight-booking status.
  • NBA Draft declaration is tracked as a 5–7-step ordered checklist that is “important because it's their eligibility” — and “nobody really knows these steps except me off the top of their head.”

Email / IT Environment

  • The firm runs on Microsoft/Outlook + GoDaddy (20–30 accounts) but the team actually operates out of personal Gmail accounts and personal Google Drives to create shared docs — which Nic calls “idiotic organization that makes me anxious.” He wants to migrate everything to Google Workspace (modeled on CrumbLabz's recent “Crosby Development” migration).

  • Google Drive (personal accounts) — ad-hoc shared docs and folder sharing.
  • Desktop folders (Nic's Mac) — de facto player document repository; the firm's real filing system.
  • Microsoft Outlook / Microsoft 365 — official email (20–30 accounts).
  • GoDaddy — domain + website (cover page only) and platform hosting.

  • Stripe — invoicing/payment links and incoming-payment visibility.
  • Claude (AI projects) — invoice generation, deck creation, on-demand spreadsheet building, the (in-progress) 5 AM stat-digest automation.
  • CBS Sports app + other consumer sports apps — live/pro & college stat tracking.
  • Twitter/X — real-time injury/news monitoring for notable players.
  • Group chats (SMS/iMessage) — recruiting coordination, intern game updates, document requests.
  • Phone contacts & notes — player/parent contact info and apparel sizes.
  • Calendar — player/family birthdays.
  • Zoom / Google Meet — calls (team is split in preference).

5 Pain Points

Recruiting & Pipeline Tracking

  • No de-duplication or coordination control. The workbook itself shows the same prospect tracked by multiple agents — e.g., Kingston Flemings (Matt and Mo), Jaylen Cross (Will and Austin Beamer), Bangot Dak (Matt and Adrian), London Jemison (Matt and Nic), Donovan Criss (Adrian and Will). This is exactly the overlap Nic fears: “we look fucking stupid if two people are reaching out to the same parent.”
  • Inconsistent data entry. Free-text spelling drift across tabs (e.g., “Kharri Coleman” vs “Kaharri Coleman”; partial/empty fields throughout) makes the data unreliable and un-queryable.
  • Stale data. The recruiting sheet “needs legit serious updating” and is only cleaned up reactively, by hand, after major events like the draft.
  • Coordination depends on a group chat + manual gatekeeping, with no audit of who contacted whom or when.

Player Information & Document Storage

  • Single point of failure / single point of knowledge. Files live on Nic's desktop and phone; “I'm usually the only one who knows exactly where everything is.” Routine requests (an address, a passport, a license) become a group-chat scramble where staff “just wait for somebody to send it.”
  • No standardization. “I would like it to be that way for everybody else” — only Nic's machine is well-organized.
  • Serious security/privacy exposure. Passports, driver's licenses, SSNs, birth certificates — and a marquee client's credit card on a shared PDF — are stored and passed around informally, with no access controls or audit trail.
  • Storage is not yet centralized (OneDrive intended but never set up).

Performance Tracking & Investor Reporting

  • The 5 AM stat email is unreliable (“not been as reliable as it was like two years ago”) and depends on a volunteer rebuilding it in spare time.
  • High-school stats are fully manual and hard to source.
  • Live tracking relies on manually coordinating 8–10 interns via sheet + chat; updates are scattered across a group thread with no consolidated record.
  • No internal equivalent of the Fringe investor dashboard yet exists for ProMondo's stakeholders.

Invoicing & Payments

  • Invoicing is “way more tedious” historically; now faster via Claude but still manual, one-off, and disconnected from the payments tracker.
  • The payments Google Sheet is maintained by hand (month-by-month owed/invoiced), with no link between Stripe activity and the tracker — reconciliation is manual.

Logistics / “Spreadsheet Bridges”

  • Event and travel tracking is rebuilt ad hoc each time (“create a spreadsheet each time”).
  • Process knowledge is trapped in one head. The eligibility-critical NBA draft-declaration sequence lives only in Nic's memory — a compliance risk if he's unavailable.

Email / IT Environment

  • The firm pays for Microsoft/Outlook + GoDaddy but works out of personal Gmail/Drive — a “broken,” “idiotic” split that creates security, continuity, and organizational risk.
  • Cross-device friction (Mac vs. PC/ThinkPad) makes simple file handoffs “really fucking annoying.”

Cost Implications

  • Paying for unused infrastructure: 20–30 Microsoft/Outlook + GoDaddy seats while the team actually works out of free personal Gmail/Drive.
  • Key-person risk has a dollar value: with one person holding all filing and process knowledge, any absence stalls travel booking, document retrieval, and draft-eligibility steps for high-value clients.
  • Reputational/recruiting cost: duplicate outreach to the same prospect/parent damages credibility and “really hurts our chances of getting whoever we get.”
  • Data-breach/liability exposure: informal handling of passports, SSNs, and a client credit card is a material legal and reputational risk as the client roster grows in profile.
  • Stripe at ~$5/invoice is modest and not a primary cost concern.

Scale of the Problem

  • Dozens of signed clients across five tiers + hundreds of active recruiting targets in 13 agent tabs — already beyond what spreadsheets + one person's desktop can safely hold.
  • 8–10 interns + ~13 agents + operations staff, all phone-first, with no shared, permissioned, mobile system.
  • The problem compounds with growth: “it only gets harder as you get bigger… doing it now is easier than doing it in two years.” The incoming Keaton Wagler engagement (full life-management for a 5th-overall NBA pick) raises both the data sensitivity and the service load immediately.

8 Constraints & Requirements

Technical Constraints

  • Mobile-first is mandatory: “everybody does do shit a lot on their phones”; “ease for computer and mobile is important.”
  • Mixed device fleet (Mac-majority, at least one Windows/ThinkPad) — must be browser/web-based, OS-agnostic.
  • Sensitive PII/financial data (passports, SSNs, licenses, credit cards) requires encryption, access controls, and an audit trail.
  • Current environment is Microsoft/Outlook + GoDaddy but the team lives in Google; a Workspace migration (20–30 accounts, ~a weekend + a day for mail to port, per CrumbLabz's Crosby Development experience) is a likely parallel track.
  • Must coexist with “spreadsheet bridges” — Nic accepts some spreadsheets will persist; centralize via links/embeds rather than forcing everything in.
  • Integrate with Stripe for invoicing/payment visibility.

Business Constraints

  • Founder resistance / low technical literacy — requires a low-friction rollout and a clear value case.
  • Budget approval sits with the founder, not Nic; pricing must be “value-based… very, very digestible.”
  • Engagement model: CrumbLabz builds first, client pays only if they adopt (“if you don't like it, you pay nothing”), then a monthly subscription covering fixes/changes/additions.
  • Seasonality: the team has bandwidth for change in summer/early fall (“August-ish… downtime”); build/rollout should target that window.
  • NDA to be executed before data sharing.

Must-Have Requirements

  • A centralized, login-based CRM / database (“its own login database CRM type of deal”) for recruiting, client roster, and player records.
  • Role-based permissions — finances visible only to select users; recruiting/player info open internally (“there's no secrecy in terms of player information”). Admin vs. basic user tiers; ability to add/remove admins.
  • Centralized document storage mirroring the existing player-folder taxonomy (Contracts, Finances, Invoices, NIL, Player Info, Pre-Draft/Combine, Travel Info), accessible to authorized staff without going through Nic.
  • Self-service data entry — staff can add players and upload documents (e.g., a passport) without CrumbLabz involvement.
  • De-duplication / coordination safeguards in recruiting to prevent two agents working the same prospect.
  • Mobile-friendly across all core functions.
  • Recruiting tracking preserving the existing tiers, statuses, “Who Has Juice?”, contacts, and dated notes — but structured and queryable.

Nice-to-Have Requirements

  • Gamified dashboards — counts of players represented, “in the hopper,” in discussions, etc.
  • Client/family-facing portal — players see their own documents; temporary guest logins surface an intro/pitch deck to recruits' families.
  • Automated performance reporting — a reliable internal version of the Fringe investor/stat digest; consolidate the 5 AM email and intern game-tracking into the platform.
  • Guided process checklists — codify the eligibility-critical NBA draft-declaration steps so the knowledge isn't trapped with Nic.
  • In-app communication tab — multi-select recipients, default/custom or AI-drafted messages, attachments (modeled on the Fringe portal).
  • Brand customization — admin can set name/colors/logo.
  • Google Workspace migration as a companion engagement.

9 Success Criteria

  • Any authorized staffer can self-serve a player's address, passport, license, sizes, or contract in seconds — eliminating group-chat document requests and removing Nic as the bottleneck (target: ~90% drop in “find me X” chat requests).
  • Zero duplicate-outreach incidents — the system flags when a prospect is already being worked by another agent before contact is made.
  • All sensitive PII and financial documents (passports, SSNs, licenses, credit cards) live in one permissioned, audited store — none circulating via chat, email, or loose PDFs.
  • One source of truth replaces the personal-Gmail/personal-Drive/desktop sprawl; new players and documents are added directly in-app by staff.
  • Finances are reliably restricted to authorized users while recruiting/player data is openly shared internally.
  • Full functionality on mobile and desktop, validated by the phone-first team in real use.
  • Recruiting data is clean and current — consistent player records, standardized statuses/tiers, no spelling-drift duplicates, updated continuously rather than in post-event cleanups.
  • Eligibility-critical processes are codified as guided checklists, executable correctly by someone other than Nic.
  • Performance/investor reporting is automated and reliable, replacing the flaky 5 AM email (benchmark: day-long → ~10-minute turnaround, as already proven with Fringe).
  • The platform is something the team voluntarily adopts — Nic's own bar: “something that I look at and see if it's something that people would hop aboard and use collectively” — and that makes the firm “look and feel way more legit” to players, families, and recruits.
