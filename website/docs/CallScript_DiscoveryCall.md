# Call Script: Discovery Call
Version 1.0

## Purpose

This is the first real conversation with a potential client. The goal is to deeply understand their operational problem, build rapport, and gather enough information for the AI to generate a strong Problem Definition Document.

**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Platform:** Google Meet (Fireflies recording active)
**Attendees:** CrumbLabz lead + client stakeholder(s)

---

## Before the Call

- [ ] Review the contact's form submission in the CRM (name, company, headache description)
- [ ] Check the company website and LinkedIn for basic context
- [ ] Ensure Fireflies.ai is connected and recording is enabled
- [ ] Name the calendar event: `[CompanyName] Discovery Call` (this enables auto-matching)
- [ ] Have the CRM open to reference their submission during the call

## Call Structure

### 1. Opening (2–3 minutes)

**Set the tone — warm, professional, and low-pressure.**

> "Hey [First Name], thanks for taking the time to chat. I'm [Your Name] from CrumbLabz. Before we dive in, I just want to set expectations — this is purely a discovery conversation. There's no pitch, no commitment. I just want to understand what's going on in your business and see if we can help. Sound good?"

**Confirm recording:**

> "Quick heads up — I have an AI meeting assistant running that records and transcribes our conversation. It helps us capture everything accurately so we can put together a really thorough analysis for you afterward. Is that okay with you?"

**If they hesitate on recording:**

> "Totally understand. I can turn it off — no problem at all. I'll just take notes manually."

### 2. Their Story (8–10 minutes)

**Let them talk. Your job is to listen.**

> "So I read what you submitted on our site — [briefly reference their headache]. Can you walk me through that in more detail? Just tell me what a typical day or week looks like when you're dealing with this."

**Follow their lead. Use these prompts if they need help expanding:**

- "Can you walk me through that process step by step?"
- "Where in that workflow do things slow down or break?"
- "How long has this been a problem?"
- "What triggers the worst days — is it volume, specific events, a particular step?"

**Listen for:**
- Manual steps that could be automated
- Data that's being moved between systems by hand
- Decisions being made without good data
- Frustration with existing tools
- Time estimates ("it takes us X hours per week")

### 3. Impact & Scale (5 minutes)

**Quantify the problem. This feeds directly into the Problem Definition Document.**

- "How often does this happen — daily, weekly?"
- "How many people on your team are affected by this?"
- "If you had to estimate, how many hours per week does this eat up?"
- "Has this ever caused a missed deadline, a lost customer, or an error that cost money?"
- "What's the ripple effect — does it slow other things down too?"

### 4. Current Tools & Attempts (3–5 minutes)

**Understand what they've already tried and what they're working with.**

- "What tools or systems are you using right now for this? Spreadsheets, specific software, email?"
- "Have you tried to solve this before — maybe with a different tool or by changing the process?"
- "If so, what worked and what didn't?"
- "Are there any tools your team loves and wouldn't want to give up?"
- "Any technical constraints I should know about — security requirements, compliance, specific platforms you're locked into?"

### 5. Vision of Success (3 minutes)

**Get them to describe what solved looks like.**

- "If you could wave a magic wand and this problem was solved tomorrow, what would that look like?"
- "What would change for your team day-to-day?"
- "How would you know it's working — what would you measure?"

### 6. Stakeholders & Decision Process (2 minutes)

**Understand who else is involved.**

- "Are you the main person who'd be using this tool, or is it more for your team?"
- "Is there anyone else who'd need to be involved in deciding to move forward?"
- "Who else should I be thinking about as a user of whatever we build?"

### 7. Close & Next Steps (3–5 minutes)

**Transition to what happens next — no pressure, just clarity.**

> "This has been really helpful, [First Name]. I feel like I have a solid understanding of what's going on. Here's what happens next:"

> "Over the next few days, I'm going to put together a Problem Definition Document — basically a structured write-up of everything we just discussed. It'll outline the problem, the current workflow, the impact, and what success looks like."

> "After that, we'll schedule a follow-up where I'll walk you through our proposed solution — what we'd build, how it would work, and what the timeline looks like. All of that is free. You don't pay anything until you've seen exactly what we're proposing and you've decided to move forward."

> "Sound good? Any questions before we wrap up?"

**Book the follow-up before hanging up if possible:**

> "Want to go ahead and pick a time for the follow-up? Usually about a week from now works well — gives us time to put together something solid."

## Key Principles

- **Listen more than you talk.** Aim for 70/30 — they should be talking 70% of the time.
- **Don't solutionize on the call.** Resist the urge to propose solutions in real time. "That's a great question — let me think about that and include it in the write-up" is a perfectly good answer.
- **Use their language.** If they call it a "tracker," don't call it a "dashboard." Mirror their vocabulary.
- **Build trust through competence.** Ask sharp, specific follow-up questions. It shows you understand their world.
- **End with certainty.** They should leave the call knowing exactly what happens next and when.
